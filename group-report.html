The Group Report is jointly authored by the members of the group, is typically of length 30,000 words (excluding appendices), and counts for 60% of the assessment. Its purpose is to document the process of delivering the agreed software, from both a technical and a process management standpoint. The report should demonstrate that the group has applied their skills in the software development process, and also that they have worked together effectively as a team.

Submission information

Every member of the group should submit a PDF of the report to Tabula along with an archive of the project source code as well as any other extras such as data sets.

Assessment Criteria

The group report is the main mechanism we have for assessing your application of good practice, both technical and managerial, to the process of delivering your project.

The following eight criteria, which carry equal weight, will be used by the assessors in marking the report.

  • Specification
    • Clear and complete problem definition.
    • Clear, precise, achievable aims and intentions.
  • Design
    • Appropriate use of a structured approach, or use of a formal or semi-formal methodology.
    • Suitable level of detail for the design documentation, including algorithms, methodologies used and identification of the boundary and scope of the project. (Justifications for the decisions taken should be included.)
    • Feasibility study.
    • Consideration of social and ethical issues (if appropriate).
    • Evidence of quality assurance, including issues of robustness, completeness and accuracy (if appropriate).
    • Opportunities for future expansion (if appropriate).
    • Portability (if appropriate).
    • Limitations.
  • Research and logical presentation of report
    • Depth of understanding - evidence of research and supporting literature.
    • Logical presentation and argument.
    • Appropriate referencing and attribution of information and diagrams.
  • Project management
    • Is there a thorough report on project management issues, including planning, organisation and scheduling. Inclusion of (for example) Gannt charts, meeting minutes, and the use of a methodology (such as prince) may be appropriate.
    • Contingency plans and actions taken.
    • Backup, recovery strategies and risk analysis.
    • Legal, social, ethical and professional issues addressed appropriately.
  • Code quality
    • Complete, correct and consistent code.
    • Code documentation.
    • Concise and non-repetitive (modular) code.
    • Readable code.
  • Testing
    • Evidence of test plans before commencement of testing.
    • Thorough testing with supporting documentation to explain the results.
    • Unit, system, acceptance, integration, and functional testing documentation (as appropriate).
  • User manual
    • Installation, configuration, usability, help (as appropriate).
    • Complete, understandable and readable.
    • Presentation as appropriate.
  • Quality of report
    • Quality of presentation.
    • Coherence, structure and degree of integration of individual contributions.
